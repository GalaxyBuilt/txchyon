## What Are Factor-Based Allocation Strategies?

Factor-based allocation is the practice of **weighting your portfolio based on measurable characteristics of assets**, rather than just gut feeling or static percentages.  

These “factors” can include momentum, volatility, market capitalization, correlation, or even value metrics. The goal is to add a **systematic, repeatable method** to how you decide what percentage of your portfolio each asset should hold.  

> Story: Alex had been investing in crypto for a year. He used to split his portfolio equally across 10 coins, picking them based on hype and social media trends. Often, he would panic-sell during sharp drops or chase recent pumps. After learning factor-based allocation, Alex started analyzing volatility, momentum, and market cap for each coin. Over time, he noticed smoother portfolio growth and fewer emotional trades, because his decisions were backed by data.

## Popular Factors for Crypto Portfolios

### 1️⃣ Momentum  
Tilt toward assets with strong recent performance.  

> Example: Last month, Solana surged 25% while other altcoins lagged. A momentum-based allocation would slightly increase Solana exposure, capturing the trend without overcommitting. Alex applied this and saw his portfolio benefit when the trend continued for several weeks.  

Related: **[Dynamic Allocation Strategies for Active Traders](https://txchyon.com/blog/portfolio-management/dynamic-allocation-strategies-for-active-traders/)**

### 2️⃣ Volatility  
High volatility increases risk.  

> Example: During a wild week, a small-cap meme coin jumped 50% in a day but dropped 30% the next. Factor-based allocation reduced exposure to such swings, shifting capital to BTC and ETH to stabilize the portfolio.  

Guides: **[Rebalancing During High-Volatility Periods](https://txchyon.com/blog/portfolio-management/rebalancing-during-high-volatility-periods/)**

### 3️⃣ Market Capitalization  
Larger, liquid assets are easier to trade and less prone to sudden manipulation.  

> Example: Alex had been tempted by a low-cap token with high growth potential. By considering market cap, he limited his position, avoiding the risk of getting stuck in an illiquid coin during a crash.  

Learn more: **[Crypto Portfolio Risk Management Explained](https://txchyon.com/blog/portfolio-management/crypto-portfolio-risk-management-explained/)**

### 4️⃣ Correlation  
Avoid overweighting assets that move together.  

> Example: Holding multiple DeFi tokens that all respond to Ethereum price swings seemed diversified. But when ETH dropped 15%, all his DeFi holdings fell similarly. Correlation-based allocation would have reduced exposure to overlapping risks, keeping the portfolio more balanced.

## How to Implement Factor-Based Allocation

1. **Select Relevant Factors**  
   Momentum, volatility, market cap, and correlation are most relevant.  

2. **Score Each Asset**  
   Quantify each factor for every coin. For example, assign a volatility score based on 30-day historical swings.  

3. **Assign Weights**  
   Increase allocation for high-scoring assets (low volatility, strong momentum) and reduce exposure to risky or weak-performing assets.  

4. **Rebalance Regularly**  
   Factor-based allocation evolves with the market. Check scores weekly or monthly (**[Rebalancing Without Selling Your Core Assets](https://txchyon.com/blog/portfolio-management/rebalancing-without-selling-your-core-assets/)**).  

5. **Track Performance**  
   Monitor how factor adjustments affect the overall portfolio: realized vs unrealized gains, drawdowns, and risk-adjusted returns (**[Tracking Crypto Portfolio Performance](https://txchyon.com/blog/portfolio-management/how-to-track-crypto-portfolio-performance/)**).

## Benefits and Risks

**Benefits:**
- Reduces emotional, reactive decisions (**[Crypto Portfolio Risk Management Explained](https://txchyon.com/blog/portfolio-management/crypto-portfolio-risk-management-explained/)**)  
- Optimizes risk-adjusted returns  
- Adapts across market cycles for flexibility  

**Risks:**
- Overfitting: factors may work historically but fail in the future  
- Ignoring trading costs: frequent rebalancing can eat gains (**[Using Stop-Loss and Take-Profit in Crypto Portfolios](https://txchyon.com/blog/portfolio-management/using-stop-loss-and-take-profit-in-crypto-portfolios/)**)  
- Neglecting overall allocation: factors complement, not replace, core strategy
